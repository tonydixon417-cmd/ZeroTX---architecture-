# The Ethics Floor
## A Cross-Cultural Foundation for AI Accountability Systems

**Contrail Equity Strategies LLC · Version 2.0 · May 2026**
**Author: Anthony Cyle Dixon**

---

**AT A GLANCE**

**THE PROBLEM:** Persistent AI systems amplify human intentions — constructive and destructive equally. No existing deployment framework establishes a non-negotiable ethical foundation below the user layer, the organizational layer, or the commercial layer.

**THE CONSEQUENCE:** Without a fixed ethical floor, a persistent AI system is a liability instrument. It will optimize for whatever objective it is given by whoever loads it first — with no structural constraint on the nature of that objective.

**THE FIX:** Five cross-culturally defensible principles — non-harm, non-deception, autonomy, fairness, and accountability — embedded as a mandatory architectural layer below all configurable system parameters. Values that do not move regardless of deployment context.

**WHO THIS IS FOR:** Organizations building, deploying, acquiring, or regulating persistent AI systems — with particular relevance to healthcare, legal, financial, and enterprise contexts where AI-assisted decisions carry institutional liability.

---

## Abstract

This paper establishes a universal ethical floor for persistent AI systems — a set of five principles that survive cross-cultural scrutiny and can serve as a non-negotiable foundation for any AI accountability framework regardless of deployment context, organizational values, or regulatory jurisdiction.

The paper does not propose a comprehensive theory of AI ethics. It proposes a minimum — a floor below which no deployment can ethically operate, and above which organizations retain full authority to define their own values, policies, and standards. The floor does not resolve every ethical question. It identifies the questions that cannot be delegated to a machine and ensures that a human being is accountable for their resolution.

Each principle is examined in operational terms, including an explicit statement of where the principle stops — where human judgment must begin and where the system's authority ends. A companion section identifies what the floor does not resolve, to prevent the framework from being applied beyond its intended scope.

This document is published to establish public prior art for the Ethics Floor concept, framework, and operational specifications as components of the broader AI accountability methodology developed by the author.

## 1. The Structural Problem

Persistent AI systems — systems that retain context across sessions, adapt to a specific user's goals and communication patterns, and operate with increasing autonomy over time — represent a qualitatively different category of risk than single-session AI tools.

A single-session AI tool produces output and resets. Its errors are contained. Its influence is bounded by the session. A persistent AI system accumulates influence. It knows what the user wants to hear. It knows which arguments are persuasive. It knows which framings produce compliance. It operates with the intimacy of sustained relationship and the authority of apparent expertise.

This is the amplification problem. A persistent AI does not merely assist — it amplifies. If the user's intentions are constructive, the system makes them more effective. If the user's intentions are destructive, the system makes those more effective too. The amplification is symmetric. The system does not discriminate.

Current AI deployment frameworks address this problem primarily through behavioral constraints — content filters, prohibited output categories, and usage policies. These operate at the surface level. They address what the system says, not what the system is structured to become over sustained deployment.

What is missing is a layer below the behavioral constraints. A set of values embedded in the architecture itself — not in the prompt, not in the policy document, not in the terms of service — that cannot be overridden by user preference, organizational pressure, or commercial incentive.

This paper defines that layer.

## 2. Why a Universal Floor Is Possible

The challenge of establishing universal ethical principles for AI is well-documented. Ethical frameworks are embedded in cultural contexts, religious traditions, and political histories that differ profoundly across the populations that will use these systems. Any framework built primarily from one tradition will be experienced as imposition by users from other traditions.

This concern is legitimate. The AI ethics literature of the past decade is marked by frameworks that presented themselves as universal and turned out to be heavily weighted toward Western liberal democratic assumptions. Researchers in non-Western contexts have documented this consistently.

The question this paper addresses is more modest: not whether a complete universal ethical system is possible, but whether a minimum set of principles exists that survives cross-cultural scrutiny. Whether there is a floor — not a ceiling, not a comprehensive framework — on which systems from any tradition can stand.

The answer, supported by decades of cross-cultural research in medical ethics, international law, developmental psychology, and comparative philosophy, is yes. A small number of principles appear consistently across traditions — not framed identically, not derived the same way, but present.

Aristotle's Nicomachean Ethics, the Buddhist principle of ahimsa, Confucian virtue ethics (ren, yi, li, zhi, xin), Ubuntu philosophy's communitarian framework, Kant's categorical imperative, and the UN Universal Declaration of Human Rights arrive at different places through different paths. On five specific principles, they converge.

## 3. The Five Principles

### 3.1 Do Not Harm

Every major ethical tradition contains a principle of non-harm. The Buddhist concept of ahimsa — non-harm as a foundational commitment — is one of the oldest formulations. Aristotle's treatment of harm as requiring justification is central to his account of justice. Islamic ethics includes mafsadah — the prevention of harm as a religious obligation. The Hippocratic tradition that shaped Western medicine rests on it. The UN Declaration of Human Rights is built around protection from harm.

The convergence is not on a definition of harm — traditions disagree on what counts as harm, who counts as a protected party, and how to weigh harms against benefits. The convergence is on the principle that harm requires justification. The default is non-harm. The burden falls on the actor who causes harm to demonstrate that it was necessary.

**Operational requirement:** The system will not assist users in actions that damage the interests of parties outside the conversation. This is not a content filter. It is a values commitment applied across the full range of situations where a user's goals might harm others. The system will surface the conflict. It will not simply comply.

**Where this principle stops:** The floor does not resolve conflicts between competing harms. When preventing one harm produces another — the surgeon who causes pain to heal, the inspector whose findings damage a transaction, the physician whose diagnosis causes harm of its own — the floor does not calculate the hierarchy of harms. That calculation belongs to a human being who can be identified and held accountable for it. The system surfaces the conflict. A person owns the resolution.

---

### 3.2 Do Not Deceive

The prohibition on deliberate deception appears across traditions with similar consistency. Confucian ethics treats honesty as one of the five cardinal virtues. Islamic ethics treats deception as among the most serious moral failures. The Kantian tradition treats deception as a categorical violation of respect for persons — to deceive is to deny another person's status as a rational agent capable of making informed decisions.

The principle is not that a system must volunteer all information at all times. It is that the system must not deliberately create false beliefs in the user's mind for the system's benefit at the user's expense.

**Operational requirement:** The system will not use knowledge of a user's psychology, preferences, or vulnerabilities to shape output toward a predetermined conclusion. The system may disagree. The system may push back. The system may deliver findings the user does not want to hear. What it will not do is manipulate rather than inform.

**Where this principle stops:** Omission is not always deception. Withholding information to protect a user's genuine interest — a physician preparing a patient before delivering a terminal diagnosis — does not cross the floor. The distinction is intent and direction: omission that serves the user does not cross the floor; omission that serves the system's interest at the user's expense does. The critical requirement: when an omission occurs, the user must be informed that an omission occurred, even if the omitted content is withheld. The user cannot consent to a gap they do not know exists. Silence about the silence is where omission becomes deception.

---

### 3.3 Respect Autonomy

Human beings have the capacity to make their own decisions. That capacity is not to be overridden without compelling justification. This principle is more contested at the margins — some traditions weight collective good more heavily than individual autonomy — but the core survives cross-cultural examination. A system that makes decisions for people without their knowledge or consent, that manipulates rather than informs, violates something that virtually every ethical tradition regards as fundamental to human dignity.

**Operational requirement:** The system is an instrument, not an autonomous agent. It informs, analyzes, synthesizes, and recommends. The decision belongs to the human. The system will not present outputs as conclusions when they are recommendations. It will not create the impression that a decision has already been made when it has not. The human is always in the loop. The loop always means something.

**Where this principle stops:** The floor does not override a user's autonomous choice to make a decision the system considers unwise — including decisions that may harm the user. Autonomy includes the right to be wrong. The system's obligation is to inform fully and flag risk clearly, without softening. Once that obligation is met, the decision belongs to the human. The system retains no veto. What it retains is a clear record that it discharged its informational duty before the decision was made.

---

### 3.4 Act Fairly

Fairness — the principle that like cases should be treated alike, that the system should not arbitrarily privilege some parties over others — appears in every major legal tradition, every religious ethical framework, and every political philosophy that has achieved significant adoption. The specifics of what fairness requires are contested. The commitment that fairness is required is not.

**Operational requirement:** The system will treat all users as ends, not means. A persistent AI deployed to a community of users — or licensed to an organization, or transferred to an acquirer — will encounter pressure to optimize for outcomes that benefit the system's owners rather than its users. The ethics floor prohibits this. The user's dignity and interests are not instrumental to another goal. They are the goal.

**Where this principle stops:** The floor does not resolve competing fairness claims. What is fair to an institution — reducing costs to survive — may be genuinely unfair to the individuals affected by that decision. Both claims may be legitimate. The floor does not adjudicate between them. What the floor requires is this: whoever made the decision using this system is on record as having made it. The fairness debate occurs above the floor. The accountability for the outcome of that debate does not. The floor is not the ceiling of fairness. It is the ground below which the conversation cannot descend — the point at which a human must own the call, regardless of how they define fair.

---

### 3.5 Be Accountable

Accountability is implicit in virtually every ethical tradition that takes consequences seriously. It is the mechanism by which the other four principles are enforced. Without accountability, the commitment to non-harm is an aspiration. With accountability, it is an obligation. Actions have consequences. The people who take actions bear responsibility for those consequences. That responsibility requires a record.

**Operational requirement:** Every significant output, every high-confidence recommendation, every decision-adjacent exchange is logged. Not surveilled — logged. The distinction is structural: surveillance serves the watcher; logging serves accountability. The record exists so that errors can be found, patterns identified, and responsibility assigned when something goes wrong. This is the AI Black Box Standard (AIBB) in ethical terms — not a technology preference, but an ethical obligation given a technical implementation.

**Where this principle stops:** A log that only the system's owner can access is not an ethics floor. It is a liability shield wearing the costume of accountability. The record must be accessible — in appropriate form, with appropriate privacy protections — to the parties affected by the decisions it documents. An audit trail that can be buried, selectively produced under legal pressure, or modified by the operator does not satisfy this principle. The test is not whether a log exists. The test is whether a person harmed by a decision made using this system can establish what the system said, when, and who approved it.

## 4. What the Floor Does Not Resolve

This section states plainly what the Ethics Floor cannot do. This is not a disclaimer. It is a precise description of the floor's design scope.

**The floor does not determine which harm is worse when two harms compete.** It requires that a human make that determination and be accountable for it.

**The floor does not define fair.** Every legal system, every religious tradition, and every political philosophy that has achieved significant adoption has produced a different answer to that question. This floor does not add another answer. It requires that whatever answer is applied, a human applied it and is on record as having done so.

**The floor does not protect individuals from their own autonomous choices.** Autonomy includes the right to make decisions others consider unwise. The floor requires the system to inform fully before the decision is made. What the individual does with that information is outside the floor's scope.

**The floor does not make AI safe in absolute terms.** No framework can make AI safe in absolute terms. The floor makes AI accountable — which is the only honest version of safety currently available.

These limitations are not weaknesses. They are honest descriptions of where human judgment begins and where it cannot be replaced. A framework that claims to resolve everything has resolved nothing — it has hidden the unresolved questions from view. This floor places the unresolved questions in plain sight, names them, and assigns them to the only entity capable of handling them: a human being who can be held responsible for the outcome.

---

## 5. What the Floor Does Not Contain

The floor does not contain a specific political framework. It does not favor democratic governance structures over others, or Western institutional models over others. Political arrangements are contested. The floor is not the place to resolve those contests.

The floor does not contain religious doctrine. The five principles were selected because they survive across religious traditions — they are not derived from any single tradition. A system built on this floor should be deployable without imposing any specific theological framework on its users.

The floor does not contain a definition of human flourishing. What constitutes the good life is genuinely contested across cultures and individuals. The floor protects the user's ability to pursue their own answer to that question — which is what respecting autonomy means in practice.

The floor does not contain the author's personal preferences. The author is an American, a Christian, a commercial pilot, and a property investor. Some of the intuitions formed by that history are correct. Some are culturally specific in ways that are not universal. The floor was deliberately constructed from frameworks that survived cross-cultural scrutiny to prevent any single formation — including the author's — from being mistaken for universal truth.

## 6. Institutional and Acquisition Implications

A persistent AI methodology built on a clearly articulated, cross-culturally defensible ethics floor is a fundamentally different product from one that is not. The difference is not visible in the feature set. It is visible in the liability profile, the regulatory posture, the institutional trust the system can generate, and the long-term defensibility of the intellectual property.

An acquiring organization — a technology company, a healthcare system, a legal services firm, a financial institution — is not purchasing a methodology when it acquires this IP. It is purchasing a framework that will be deployed to its users, in its name, under its regulatory obligations. The ethics floor is the component that determines whether that deployment reduces institutional liability or creates it. Whether it builds or erodes the institutional trust that consequential AI deployment requires.

The people who make acquisition decisions are not philosophers. They are risk managers. A demonstrably sound, cross-culturally defensible ethics floor with explicit statements of scope and limitation is among the most significant risk management assets in an AI accountability IP package — because it demonstrates that the framework was built to survive hostile scrutiny, not just favorable conditions.

The floor was built to last. Everything deployed above it depends on it.

## 7. Relationship to the Broader Accountability Stack

The Ethics Floor is one component of a layered AI accountability architecture. Its relationship to the other components is structural:

- The **AI Black Box Standard (AIBB)** provides the technical logging infrastructure that makes the Accountability principle operational.
- The **Loop Detector** identifies the failure modes — Abdication, Consequence, Responsibility Diffusion, and Mode Confusion loops — that the floor's principles are designed to prevent at the values level.
- The **Personal Identity Layer (PIL)** defines the persistent context architecture through which the floor's principles are maintained across sessions and deployments.
- The **Drift Taxonomy** provides the diagnostic vocabulary for identifying when a deployed system has deviated from the floor's requirements.

None of these components replaces the others. The floor without logging is aspiration. Logging without a floor is surveillance. The stack is the system. The floor is what the stack rests on.
